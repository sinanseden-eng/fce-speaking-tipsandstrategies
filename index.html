FCE Speaking Guide

Your key to success! Here are the essential language structures and vocabulary to help you excel in your B2 speaking assessment.

Key Language Structures

üó£Ô∏è Expressing & Justifying Opinions

This is the most fundamental skill. You need to clearly state what you think and *why*.

  • "In my opinion..."

    e.g., "In my opinion, exams aren't the best way to assess students."

  • "I'm convinced that..."

    e.g., "I'm convinced that learning a second language is crucial today."

  • "As I see it..."

    e.g., "As I see it, practical skills are just as important as academic subjects."

  • "This is because..."

    e.g., "...it teaches discipline. This is because you have to train every day."

  • "The main reason for this is..."

    e.g., "The main reason for this is that it opens up new job opportunities."

  • "For instance, take..."

    e.g., "Many sports bring people together. For instance, take the World Cup."

Example:

"In my opinion, it's essential for young people to learn practical skills. This is because not everyone goes to university, and skills like managing money are vital for everyday life."

üîÑ Comparing & Contrasting

You will often need to discuss two different things (e.g., online vs. in-store shopping).

  • "...is more/less [adjective] than..."

    e.g., "Shopping online is more convenient than going to a mall."

  • "...is not as [adjective] as..."

    e.g., "Driving a car is not as eco-friendly as using public transport."

  • "On the one hand,..."

    e.g., "On the one hand, living in a city is exciting."

  • "On the other hand,..."

    e.g., "On the other hand, it can be very noisy and expensive."

  • "In contrast,..."

    e.g., "Big stores have a lot of choice. In contrast, small shops offer personal service."

  • "Whereas..."

    e.g., "Whereas fashion trends change quickly, personal style is more permanent."

Example:

"On the one hand, shopping in stores lets you see the quality. On the other hand, online shopping is often more convenient."

üí° Making Suggestions & Giving Advice

Many questions will ask how a problem can be solved or what *should* be done.

  • "I think people should..."

    e.g., "I think people should try to reduce their plastic waste."

  • "Governments ought to..."

    e.g., "Governments ought to provide free access to healthcare for everyone."

  • "It would be a good idea if..."

    e.g., "It would be a good idea if schools taught more practical skills."

  • "One solution could be to..."

    e.g., "One solution could be to build more parks in cities."

  • "One way to... is to..."

    e.g., "One way to tackle pollution is to improve public transport."

Example:

"To promote a healthy lifestyle, I think governments should invest more in public parks. It would also be a good idea to run campaigns about nutrition."

üîÆ Speculating (Future & Hypothetical)

Talk about the future or imaginary situations (use the Second Conditional).

  • "It's likely that... will..."

    e.g., "It's likely that robots will replace people in many jobs."

  • "It's possible that... might..."

    e.g., "It's possible that we might find new ways to generate clean energy."

  • "I doubt that... will..."

    e.g., "I doubt that people will ever stop listening to music."

  • "If people [past tense], it would [verb]..."

    e.g., "If people recycled more, it would have a positive impact on the environment."

Example:

"It's likely that technology will continue to change how we learn. If schools had more funding, they could provide a tablet for every student."

üîó Describing Cause & Effect

Explain *how* one thing affects another (e.g., the impact of social media).

  • "This leads to..."

    e.g., "Relying on social media for news can lead to misunderstandings."

  • "This results in..."

    e.g., "Poor work-life balance often results in stress and burnout."

  • "...has a significant impact on..."

    e.g., "Technology has a significant impact on how students learn today."

  • "...is caused by..."

    e.g., "A lot of unhappiness at work is caused by poor management."

  • "...has transformed the way we..."

    e.g., "The internet has transformed the way we communicate and shop."

Example:

"The rise of social media has had a huge impact on how people get news. This can lead to the spread of misinformation."

‚öñÔ∏è Discussing Pros & Cons

Show you can make a balanced argument by discussing both sides of an issue.

  • "The main advantage of... is..."

    e.g., "The main advantage of remote work is flexibility."

  • "A key benefit is..."

    e.g., "A key benefit of having a pet is the companionship they provide."

  • "The primary drawback is..."

    e.g., "The primary drawback of online shopping is not being able to try things on."

  • "On the downside..."

    e.g., "On the downside, living in a big city can be very expensive."

  • "The pros and cons are..."

    e.g., "When considering a career change, you must weigh all the pros and cons."

Example:

"The main advantage of public transport is reducing pollution. However, a significant drawback is that it can be unreliable."

üîÑ Paraphrase the Question

Don't repeat the question. Show your range by using different words.

IF ASKED ABOUT:

"staying healthy"

YOU CAN SAY:

"maintaining your well-being" or "leading an active lifestyle"

IF ASKED ABOUT:

"protecting the environment"

YOU CAN SAY:

"environmental conservation" or "being eco-friendly"

üó£Ô∏è Use Discourse Markers

Use these "signposting" words to structure your answer and sound more fluent.

  • To add a point: "Furthermore...", "Moreover...", "In addition..."
  • To show contrast: "However...", "Although...", "Despite this..."
  • To conclude: "So, in summary...", "Overall, I'd say..."
